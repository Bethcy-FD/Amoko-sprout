How to make FUFU from cassava tubers?

Fufu, which is made by fresh cassava tubers and eaten with soups like Egusi, Okro soup and other delicious food. In Africa, cassava tubers is abundant, local people often process cassava to garri, cassava flour, cassava starch and fufu flour etc to meet daily consumption.
Cassava fufu contains much starch which benefits the digestive tract and reduces bloating and inflammation. It feeds good bacteria and aid good digestion. This is why cassava fufu flour is popular in local. So, today Amoko's Sprout introduce the process of making cassava fufu to you from the perspective of tradition.

These are main processing steps about fufu processing:

Phase 1: Fermentation and Processing the Dough

  • 1. Peel and wash the cassava: Use a sharp knife to peel off the thick brown outer skin and the thin inner layer to expose the white flesh. Wash the peeled tubers thoroughly.
  • 2. Soak the cassava: Cut the peeled cassava into smaller sizes and place them in a large container. Cover the container in clean water and leave it in a warm place to ferment for 3 - 5 days.This process helps to remove harmful substances and softens the cassava.
  • 3. Check for fermentation: The cassava is properly fermented when it is soft to the touch and gives off a characteristic pungent, sour smell.
  • 4. Sieve the cassava: Drain the water from the fermented cassava. In a large bowl, place a sieve or chiffon cloth and gradually put small quantities of the soft cassava into it. Rub and massage the cassava with your hands, adding a little clean water if needed, to pass the fine fufu paste through the sieve into the bowl, leaving the tough fibers (chaff) behind.
  • 5. Dewater the paste: Allow the sieved liquid to settle for a few hours. Carefully pour off the clear water on top, leaving the wet, solid fufu paste behind.
  • 6. Squeeze out excess water: Transfer the wet paste into a sack. Tie it tightly and place it on a surface where the remaining water can drain out. Place a heavy object on top of the bag to press out the moisture like wooden plank. This process continues until the fufu paste feels solid to touch (leave overnight).

Phase 2a: Cooking the Fufu (IGBO)

  • 1a. Form raw fufu balls: Break the dewatered, raw fufu paste into smaller pieces. Add a little water to form a smooth paste and mold it into small, manageable balls and wrap in a nylon.
  • 2a. First cook: Bring a pot of water to a boil. Place the raw fufu balls into the boiling water, cover the pot, and cook for about 40 minutes.
  • 3a. First pounding: Remove the balls from the pot and transfer them to a mortar. Pound vigorously with a pestle until all lumps are crushed and the fufu is smooth and consistent in texture.
  • 4a. Second cook: Mold the now smooth fufu into a round shape and place it back into the same pot of boiling water. Cover and cook for another 25-30 minutes.
  • 5a. Final pounding and shaping: Remove the fufu from the pot. Pound it again until it is very smooth. Add hot water from the pot as needed during pounding to achieve the desired soft, pliable consistency.
  • 6a. Serve: Mold the finished fufu into individual serving balls. It can be wrapped in wrapping nylon to retain moisture and can be served hot with any Nigerian soup of choice, such as egusi or bitterleaf soup.

Phase 2b: Cooking the Fufu (YORUBA)

  • 1b. Place the dewatered dough with some added water to form a paste-like consistency into a clean pot on medium heat.
  • 2b. Begin stirring vigorously with a wooden spatula (omorogun) , continuously mixing as the mixture heats up and thickens to prevent lumps from forming.
  • 3b. Keep stirring until the fufu changes from bright white to an off-white or creamy color and becomes a thick, smooth dough.
  • 4b. As it becomes hard to stir, add a little more water, cover the pot, and let it steam/cook thoroughly for another 5-10 minutes on low heat.
  • 5b. Stir one last time until it achieves a smooth, consistent texture.
  • 6b. Serve: Mold the finished fufu into individual serving balls. It can be wrapped in wrapping nylon to retain moisture and can be served hot with any Nigerian soup of choice, such as egusi or bitterleaf soup.
